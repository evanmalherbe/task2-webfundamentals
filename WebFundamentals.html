Task 2 - Web Fundamentals

Ques 1 - What is Web 3.0 and how is it different from Web 1.0 and Web 2.0?

Answer: Web 3.0 is the next generation of the world wide web. While it is still a little unclear exactly what shape this new evolution of the web will take, it will certainly make more use of Artificial Intelligence to provide more relevant content to web users. Users will have complete control over their own data, thanks to encryption algorithms. This is in contrast to the situation at the moment, where huge companies like Facebook own the user's data.

This is different from Web 1.0, which consisted of mainly static web pages that simply presented information and images to the users, but did not allow the users to interact with or add to the web page at all. All they could do was view the content and then leave.

Web 2.0, which is the stage we are currently in, allows users to upload their own content to websites. This allows websites to benefit from the knowledge and expertise of millions of people (e.g. Wikipedia). This stage also gave rise to things like Blogs, where people could share information, images and videos from their own lives or of their experiences. Social media was also born here, giving people around the world the ability to interact with other people around the world and share their lives.

Ques 2 - What are the functional differences between the front-end of a web application and its back-end?

Answer: The front-end of a website is the part that you see on the page. In other words, the text, images and videos on the page. These are build and styled using HTML, CSS and Javascript languages. The back-end refers to the logic and information handling that is done behind the scenes, on the server and with databases. This part of the website is coded using languages such as Python, Ruby and PHP.

Ques 3 - In your own words, explain the process that takes place from when you type a URL into the address bar in your browser until you finally view the page you have requested.

Answer: When you have typed the URL in the browser's address bar, the request for the website is sent to your router, it then goes to a server at your ISP (internet service provider). Your ISP requests the specific web page from the actual website server. The page is sent to the ISP and then to your router and finally, to your computer's browser.

What one should think about before adding images to your website

It is critically important not to use images that are protected by copyright laws on your website. Only use images that you own personally, that you have got permission to use from the owner (written), or ones that are in the public domain (i.e. they can legally be used by anyone without specific permission)
